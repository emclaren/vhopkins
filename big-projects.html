#1. Visualizing Polarization on the Environment in the US

Environmental discussions on Twitter are highly polarized

Democrats talk about
pollution & climate change

Republicans talk about
taxes & jobs

Social media is an increasingly important part of public debate. Behavioural scientists should understand how social media can encourage biased information search—that is, citizens’ selective exposure to ideologically congruent rhetoric.

Here, I combine webscraping, Natural Language Processing, and Social Network Analysis, and to illustrate political polarization about the environment in the US.

First, I used Twitter’s API to identify users who mention either “Democrat” or “Liberal” and “Republican” or “Conservative” in their Twitter bio. Among these users, I scraped more than 1.2 million tweets discussing the environment between June 2017 and April 2018. For example, I collected tweets mentioning hashtags like #Recycle, #Green and #Environment.

Second, I applied a Structural Topic Model to identify the most commonly co-occurring words. This is a fast, easy way to sort large text corpora into a few important categories. For example, the “Pollution” category includes words like “ClimateChange, Pollution, DirtyCoal, Toxic”. The “Jobs” category includes words like “CleanCoal, CarbonTax, Socialism, Taxes”.

Third, I turn this data into a network. Two tweets share a tie if they mention the same topic. I colour each tweet by the party of the account (blue for Democrat, red for Republican) and size each tweet by the number of retweets.

The results are purely descriptive, but they help visualize the “echo chamber” in which environmental discussions often take place (at least in the US).
